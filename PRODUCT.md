# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

## Users

**Primary audience**: local diners in the restaurant's vicinity looking for a premium Indian tandoor-and-grill reservation. **Secondary audience**: food enthusiasts — the kind of diner who values the craft behind the meal and enjoys exchanging food knowledge from across the world. The space serves both: one table may hold a quiet date night, the next a spirited debate about spice routes. The page addresses both without splitting its voice.

## Product Purpose

Bonfire BBQ exists to make you hungry. The landing page is the first course — a sensory tease of coal smoke, tandoor glow, and the promise of meat cooked with conviction. Success is a clicked reservation and a diner who arrives already excited.

## Positioning

Indian coal & clay oven craft where ancient spice routes meet modern charcoal discipline. Not a generic BBQ joint — a professional kitchen built around the tandoor and the open flame, serving food that tastes like someone cared enough to learn the fire.

## Brand Personality

**Three words**: meat, professionalism, elegant. The voice lives between two poles — primal enough to make you crave charred bone marrow, polished enough to trust the reservation system. The page communicates hunger through atmosphere (smoke, ember glow, tandoor heat) and trust through precision (clean borders, deliberate typography, zero clutter).

## Anti-references

No specific anti-references identified. General intent: not a generic steakhouse slideshow, not rustic wooden boards and chalkboard menus, not a corporate chain template. The existing Verge-inspired dark editorial system already avoids those lanes.

## Design Principles

1. **Primal hunger meets polished craft** — every visual choice either provokes appetite or communicates professional confidence. If it does neither, it doesn't belong.
2. **The darkness is the stage, not the theme** — `#131313` canvas isn't dark mode; it's the visual equivalent of a candlelit dining room. The page uses darkness to make the fire (mint accent, tandoor glow, coal ember imagery) feel like the only light source.
3. **Color is fire, not decoration** — Jelly Mint and Verge Ultraviolet aren't brand colors in the traditional sense. One is the ember, the other is the smoke. They appear as hazard accents precisely because fire demands attention.
4. **One narrative, one scroll** — the single-page structure isn't a technical constraint; it's a deliberate pacing. Hero fire → history of the craft → the dishes → the atmosphere → the reservation. Every section is a course.
5. **No safety moves** — no gradients, no box-shadows, no glassmorphism, no generic cards. The Verge-inspired system already enforces this. Every pixel earns its place.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG AA as a baseline. The dark canvas (`#131313`) with white text passes contrast trivially. Mint accents (`#3cffd0`) on dark backgrounds must never be used for body text under 16px (vibrates at small sizes). Every animation (GSAP-driven) must respect `prefers-reduced-motion: reduce` with a crossfade-or-instant alternative. Keyboard navigation and focus indicators (cyan `#1eaedb`) are non-negotiable. Color-blind accessibility considered: mint and ultraviolet differ in luminance as well as hue, so meaning isn't carried by color alone.
